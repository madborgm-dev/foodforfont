The History of Croquetas de bacalao & Valentina

Known as “a little bolster of flavored béchamel encased in a crisp jacket of finely grated bread crumbs,” this dish can be traced back to Andalucía, a region of hills, rivers and farmland bordering Spain’s southern coast. Croquetas are a staple of most tapas bars, and a way for Spanish housewives to demonstrate their skills without having to buy many expensive ingredients. They may be filled with shrimp, mushrooms, leftover chicken or fish, or serrano ham bone scraps.

Valentina is a classic didone typeface designed following the rules set by Bodoni on the 18th Century. The classic design is combined with many features from the antique Spanish punches of that period. The type designer, Pedro Arilla, is originally from Spain, and named the font after his grandmother. The typeface features over 450+ glyphs and 46 different ligatures.

Recipe

Ingredients

three fourth cup milk
five garlic cloves, chopped
300 g. wet-salted cod filets
one sebago potato, steamed & mashed
two tbsp. extra virgin olive oil
one lemon, zested & one tsp juice
two roma tomatoes, concasse
two eggs, lightly beaten with one tbsp of milk
plain flour & panko breadcrumbs
small sebago potato, steamed with skin on, peeled & mashed
four garlic cloves, crushed
150 mL extra virgin olive oil

Steps

For alioli, combine potato and garlic.

While whisking, gradually add oil, drop by drop at first, then in a thin, steady stream until it’s emulsified.

Place milk, garlic and cod in a small pan over medium heat and cook for 10 minutes or until cod is tender.

Drain the mixture, reserving the solids.

Add potato, olive oil, lemon zest and juice, and cream.

Shape into 14 oval croquettes, then refrigerate for 30 minutes or until firm.

Dredge croquettes in flour, shake off excess, then dip in egg and coat in breadcrumbs.

Fill a deep-fryer or large pan 1/3 full with oil and heat to 180°C.

Gently drop into oil and fry. Serve with your alioli.
