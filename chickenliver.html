The History of Chicken Liver Mousse & Cochin

Chicken Liver Mousse is one of the first recipes aspiring chefs learn to make. The traditional French dish was a staple in Julia Child’s Mastering the Art of French Cooking. The ingredient “liver” may be a little worrisome for the average foodie, but it’s rich history, and rich taste are very approachable, similar to the French transitional typeface, Cochin.

Cochin, was produced in 1912 by Georges Peignot for his Paris type founder. The typeface was designed on the copperplate engravings of the French 17th century artist Nicolas Cochin, the typeface’s namesake.

Recipe

Ingredients

two tbsp. butter
two cups chopped onion
one cup chopped tart apple
one pound chicken livers
one fourth tsp. white pepper
one half tsp. salt
one fourth cup brandy
one cup heavy cream

Steps

In a large sauce pan over low heat, melt the butter and cook onion, apple, and thyme leaves covered.

When apples are softened, remove lid and increase heat to medium level.

Add cut chicken livers to the pan and cook until they are firm yet still pink inside.

Remove the chicken livers, onion, apple, and thyme leaves from the heat and allow to cool.

Add the white pepper, salt, and brandy into a food processor and puree all ingredients.

After pureeing all the ingredients in a food processor, cover and chill until firm.

While the puree is chilling, whip the heavy cream to medium peaks. Fold the peaks into the cooled pureed liver mixture.

Serve chilled. We also recommend a fresh, french baguette.
